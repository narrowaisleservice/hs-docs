Excessive demands made of workers Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • Employees are provided with adequate and achievable demands in relation to the agreed hours of work

    • Personal work plans ensure workers know what their job involves

    • Weekly team meetings discuss anticipated workload and forthcoming week (and to deal with planned absence)

    • Regular meetings with workers (one to one) to discuss workload and any anticipated challenges

    • Workload is adjusted to cope with peaks and absence cover in advance and with involvement of workers

    • Workers are provided with sufficient resources (time and equipment) to complete their work

    • Additional support provided to those with less experience or under pressure

    • Training is provided to workers to allow them to time manage

    • Information provided to workers to allow them to seek help if they have conflicting priorities

    • Working hours and shift patterns have been agreed with workers

    • Some amount of flexibility in start and finish times is available, to allow workers to manage outside work arrangements (commuting / childcare)

    • System in place to notify workers if short deadline work is taken on, requiring longer hours for a short period

    • Regular breaks are provided to workers

    • Realistic deadlines are placed on workers

  • The skills and abilities of workers are matched to the job demands

    • Skills / Training matrix is maintained to ensure workers are allocated tasks they are competent for

    • Workers assist in developing Development plans to identify training requirements

    • Training forms a part of the performance monitoring arrangements we have in place to ensure it is effective and sufficient

    • Team leaders empowered not to take on work for their team if capacity is reached

    • Workers are not contacted ‘out of hours’

  • Jobs are designed to be within the capabilities of workers

    • Jobs provide stimulation and opportunities for workers to use their skills to maintain motivation

  • Concerns raised by workers about their work environment are addressed

    • Risk assessments for the environment are kept up to date and controls maintained to reduce distraction (noise, dust, vibration, etc.)

    • Controls in place to manage the risk of verbal abuse and physical violence to workers

    • Training provided to staff to de-escalate difficult situations

Lack of control over work Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • Workers have some control over the pace of their work

    • Weekly team meetings discuss anticipated workload and forthcoming week (and to deal with planned absence)

    • Regular meetings with workers (one to one) to discuss workload

    • Discussion forums at the start of a project to identify working methods and output

    • Teams created for specific projects and responsibility allocated to Team, rather than individuals

  • Workers are encouraged to use their skills and initiative to do their work

    • Regular meetings with workers (one to one) to discuss skills and development

  • Where possible, workers are encouraged to develop new skills to help them undertake new and challenging pieces of work

  • Workers are supported to develop their skills

  • Workers are consulted on when breaks are taken

  • Workers are consulted on their work patterns

  • Decision making process is discussed and workers involved

Lack of support Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • Policy and procedure in place to support workers

  • Managers are enabled and encouraged to support staff

    • Weekly team meetings discuss anticipated workload and forthcoming week, as well as work-related stress

    • Regular meetings with workers (one to one) to discuss work-related stress

    • Workers consulted on how they would like to access management support

  • Workers are enabled and encouraged to support colleagues

    • Examples of good practice are shared across the team

  • Support is available and details on how and when to access it is understood by staff

    • Posters displayed with contact details for support organisations

  • Some amount of flexibility in start and finish times is available, to allow workers to manage outside work arrangements (commuting / childcare)

  • Workers know how to access resources required to do their job

  • Workers have regular and constructive feedback

  • Training forms a part of the performance monitoring arrangements we have in place to ensure it is effective and sufficient

Lack of positive working relationships Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • We actively promote positive behaviours at work, to avoid conflict and ensure fairness

  • Workers share information relative to their work

  • Policy and procedures for dealing with unacceptable behaviour, including zero tolerance for discrimination of any kind, communicated to all workers

  • Managers are provided with mechanisms to deal with unacceptable behaviour

  • Workers have a confidential mechanism to report unacceptable behaviour

  • Good communication is encouraged, through training and skill development (listening skills and confidence building)

  • Teams are selected with a good balance of expertise and experience when preparing for new projects

  • Success is celebrated at all levels of the organisation

Poor understanding of role and/or responsibilities Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • Our structure ensures that the different requirements we place on workers are compatible

    • Weekly team meetings discuss roles and any conflicts between roles

    • Regular meetings with workers (one to one) to review specific standards of performance and individual tasks

    • Personal work plans ensure workers know what their job involves

    • Induction process includes explanation of worker role and responsibilities and ensure team members are aware of them

    • Changes to roles and responsibilities (e.g. through promotion / re-organisation) are clarified to all team members

  • Information is provided to workers to enable them to understand their role and responsibilities

  • Workers are provided with clear information about the requirements of their role

  • Workers have a mechanism to report any concerns or uncertainties they have about their role or responsibilities

Poor management and/or communication of change Stress and/or mental health issues E 4 3 12

  • Information about proposed changes are communicated to workers (via meetings, email, noticeboards, leaflets, etc.) in a timely manner

    • Developments are communicated as early and as quickly as possible

  • Workers are consulted on proposed changes early on, with an opportunity to influence proposals

    • Mechanism provided for staff to ask questions before, during and after change

  • Workers are provided with information about changes to their jobs and supported with training, where necessary

  • On each occasion, we set out a timetable for change and communicate it (via email, noticeboards, leaflets, etc.) to workers

  • Workers have access to support throughout the change process

  • Personal work plans are reviewed as part of the change process and formally after change is implemented
